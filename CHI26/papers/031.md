**Title:** The 2025 State of Research Repositories: An Industry Report
**Authors:** Marvin (heymarvin.com) with expert contributors: Jake Burghardt, Emily Chee, Emily DiLeo, Johanna Jagow, Kate Pazoles, Beth Seaman, Dr. Ari Zelmanow
**Source:** Marvin Industry Report, 2025
**One-liner:** A qualitative analysis of 360 research repository reviews plus expert interviews reveals four reasons why most repositories fail — and what the successful ones actually do differently.

---

## TL;DR — Why You Should Care

Here's a number that should haunt anyone building a qualitative research platform: researchers routinely spend weeks or months conducting studies that no one else in their company ever reads. The insights get filed, the project gets closed, and the next team starts from scratch asking the same questions. This report from Marvin — the AI research repository platform — is essentially an industry-wide diagnosis of that problem.

The methodology is honest and appropriately qualitative for a qualitative-research company: they analyzed three hundred and sixty reviews of research repository tools, conducted exclusive interviews with seven leading experts in research operations and user research leadership, and layered in passive behavioral data from their own platform. The result is four findings that explain why most repositories become graveyards of PDFs rather than living infrastructure for decision-making.

What makes this worth your time isn't the findings themselves — any experienced researcher will nod along — it's the framing. Jake Burghardt, author of "Stop Wasting Research," articulates it cleanly: a research repository is a huge opportunity to present a point of view that turns research from an optional input into an active and trusted collaborator. That reframe — from storage to influence — is the entire design challenge.

---

## The Core Argument

The report argues that research repositories fail not because of technology but because of organizational and cultural preconditions that technology can't fix. You can have the best-in-class AI insight retrieval system, but if the organization hasn't already bought into research-led decision-making, the repository becomes an expensive filing cabinet. This is their first and most sobering finding: culture eats repository strategy for breakfast.

But the report is not fatalistic. It positions AI-native platforms as the mechanism that lowers the cultural barrier. When you no longer need to understand complex research methodologies to find relevant insights — when anyone can just ask a natural-language question and get an evidence-based answer — adoption becomes possible at scale. The trained researcher stops being the bottleneck between raw data and business decisions.

The other two findings — adoption strategy and decision impact — are really two sides of the same coin. You need a change management strategy to get people using the repository consistently, and that usage only becomes valuable when it actually changes what decisions get made. A repository that gets used but never influences anything is just a more expensive version of the PDF graveyard.

## Key Insights and Evaluation

**The maturity prerequisite is real and under-discussed.** The report's first finding — that companies with low research maturity need to fix that before investing in a repository — is genuinely important because most repository vendors skip this conversation. They'll sell you the tool without asking whether your organization has the cultural substrate to use it. This finding reframes repository failure as an organizational design problem, not a product problem.

**AI as a democratization lever, not just an efficiency play.** The most interesting framing in the report is that AI-assisted repositories help everyone — not just trained researchers — surface customer insights without needing to understand research methodology. This is a fundamentally different value proposition than "AI helps researchers work faster." It's "AI removes the researcher as a required intermediary," which changes who benefits and, consequently, who has incentive to fund and maintain the system.

**The adoption finding points at something deeper.** The report says success requires time, consistency, and a cohesive change management strategy. This is true, but it's also a polite way of saying that repository adoption is a behavior change problem, not a features problem. The most technically impressive repository will fail if researchers don't have incentive structures that reward sharing, and if stakeholders don't have workflows that pull them toward evidence.

**What the report doesn't say** is worth noting. It doesn't surface specific adoption rates, time-to-value data, or comparative analysis between AI-native and traditional repository approaches. The methodology — qualitative analysis of user reviews — is sound for surfacing themes but can't tell you how common each failure mode is, or whether the AI scalability finding is supported by outcome data. It's diagnostic, not prescriptive with evidence.

## Seena Labs Relevance

This report is essentially a market validation document for Seena, and you should read it that way. The central problem it diagnoses — research that gets conducted but never used, insights that arrive too late to influence decisions, repositories that require expert mediation to be useful — is precisely the gap Seena is designed to close.

The culture-first finding creates an interesting positioning question for Seena. If organizations with low research maturity can't benefit from a repository, does that mean Seena should only target high-maturity organizations? Or does it mean Seena's design — AI-mediated micro-interviews embedded in product workflows — sidesteps the maturity requirement entirely? There's a meaningful difference between selling a repository to an organization that already does research and embedding a research function into product workflows for organizations that currently do none. Seena may be solving the maturity problem by making research so lightweight and continuous that it doesn't require organizational transformation to begin.

The AI democratization finding maps directly to Seena's value proposition. If the barrier to repository value is that only trained researchers can extract insights from it, then a system that makes insight retrieval accessible to product managers, engineers, and executives without methodological knowledge becomes a fundamentally different product. Seena's AI interviewer and automated analysis pipeline is exactly this: it makes research participation possible for people who would never run a traditional study, and it makes insight consumption possible for stakeholders who would never read a research report.

The adoption finding is the most tactical. Every repository product lives or dies by consistent usage, and the report is clear that features alone don't drive it. For Seena, this means the question of how insights get pushed to stakeholders — rather than waiting for stakeholders to pull them — is a product design question, not a marketing question. **The research that gets used is the research that's already in the workflow when a decision gets made**, not the research that lives in a separate tool someone has to remember to open.

## Everything is Designed — Social Media Angle

Here's the dinner party version: most companies are paying for a library that nobody reads. They hire researchers, run studies, generate insights — and then those insights go into a folder that no one opens again until the next researcher joins and wonders if this has ever been studied before. It has. Twice. Three years ago. By the person who left.

The 2025 State of Research Repositories is essentially a post-mortem on an entire industry's infrastructure problem, and the diagnosis is uncomfortable: the technology was never the issue. The issue is that organizations bought repository software before they had a culture of actually using research to make decisions. You can't file your way to research maturity. You have to build the organizational muscle first.

What's shifting now is that AI might let companies skip part of that muscle-building. When anyone in the company can ask a plain-language question and get a research-backed answer — without needing a researcher to run a project, synthesize data, and write a report — the barrier to using research drops dramatically. The researcher stops being a gatekeeper and becomes an infrastructure builder. That's a profound shift in what it means to be a UX researcher, and this report is one of the clearer early maps of where that shift is going.
