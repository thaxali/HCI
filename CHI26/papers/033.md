**Title:** Can AI Be Pro-Worker?
**Author:** John Cassidy
**Source:** The New Yorker, The Financial Page · March 3, 2026
**One-liner:** Three economists argue that automation versus augmentation is a deliberate design choice, and that policy can tip the scales toward workers.

---

## TL;DR — Why You Should Care

Here's the uncomfortable truth that most AI coverage buries: the question of whether artificial intelligence destroys jobs or enhances them is not primarily a technological question. It is a political and economic one. John Cassidy, writing in The New Yorker's Financial Page, makes this case by reporting on a new framework from three of the most credentialed economists in the world — Daron Acemoglu, David Autor, and Simon Johnson, all at MIT — who have spent years studying how technology reshapes labor markets.

Their argument, published as part of a Brookings Hamilton Project paper, is deceptively simple: there is no iron law that says AI must replace workers. The choice between building AI that automates tasks and AI that amplifies human capability is made by engineers, product managers, and executives every single day. And right now, the market is systematically choosing automation — not because it creates more value, but because it's easier to measure, easier to justify in a board deck, and because the incentive structures of most organizations reward headcount reduction over capability expansion.

Cassidy's framing is important because he reaches a general audience with what has largely been an academic debate. As fears of mass unemployment grow louder — with AI displacing entry-level jobs in tech, finance, and law — this article serves as a mainstream articulation of the "third way": neither techno-optimism nor doomism, but deliberate steering of a technology that is, as the economists argue, genuinely dual-use.

---

## The Core Argument

Acemoglu, Autor, and Johnson introduce a taxonomy of five technology types that is worth holding onto. They distinguish between labor-augmenting technologies (which make workers more productive), capital-augmenting technologies (which improve machines), automating technologies (which replace human work entirely), expertise-leveling technologies (which democratize specialist knowledge), and new task-creating technologies (which generate roles that didn't exist before).

Of these five, only new task-creating technologies are "unambiguously pro-worker" — because they generate demand for novel human expertise rather than commodifying existing skills. Expertise-leveling technologies have a mixed record: democratizing a skill sounds good, but it can also commodify what used to be a premium service. Think about how real-time translation apps both helped non-English speakers and hollowed out the professional interpreter market.

The paper identifies a market failure at the heart of current AI development. Firms perceive greater short-term returns from automation — you can measure a displaced worker's salary, but you cannot easily measure the upside of a more capable workforce. This is the same bias that leads companies to cut training budgets during downturns: the cost is visible, the benefit is diffuse. The economists propose nine policy interventions to correct this failure, ranging from targeted tax incentives to antitrust enforcement to giving workers a formal voice in how AI gets deployed in their workplaces.

## Key Insights and Evaluation

The strongest insight in Cassidy's piece is also the most politically inconvenient: **the automation bias in current AI development is not neutral market efficiency — it's the result of deliberate choices that happen to favor capital over labor.** When a healthcare company builds an AI diagnostic tool that replaces radiologists rather than one that makes radiologists faster and more accurate, that is a design decision. When a law firm deploys document review AI to eliminate junior associates rather than to help those associates handle more complex work, that too is a choice.

**Cassidy does a good job surfacing the counterargument**, citing Joshua Gans's critique that the Acemoglu-Autor-Johnson proposal runs into a fundamental contradiction: you cannot simultaneously argue that markets are failing to develop pro-worker AI (implying it would be profitable if they tried) and that firms need tax incentives to do so (implying it's actually less profitable). This tension is real and the paper doesn't fully resolve it.

What the article gets right is the stakes. The window for shaping AI's labor market trajectory may be short. Once automation infrastructure gets built into enterprise systems, once the workforce planning models get calibrated around reduced headcounts, the path dependency becomes real. Getting the defaults right now matters more than any corrective policy after the fact.

The weakest part of Cassidy's coverage is that it remains high-altitude. He describes the framework clearly but doesn't do much with the specific policy mechanisms — the DARPA-style competitive prizes, the "expertise theft" legal protections, the antitrust angle. These are where the rubber meets the road and where the paper earns its policy credentials. For those details, go straight to the Brookings paper (#32 in this reading list).

## Why This Matters

For anyone building AI products, this framework reframes your design decisions as political acts. **Every AI feature you ship either augments or automates.** The choice to build a tool that helps a researcher conduct twenty interviews instead of five, versus a tool that eliminates the researcher's role entirely, is a pro-worker or anti-worker design decision. You make these choices constantly without necessarily naming them that way.

The Acemoglu-Autor-Johnson lens is also useful for positioning in a market that increasingly cares about AI ethics and workforce impact. The emerging regulatory environment in the EU and, increasingly, in some U.S. states is moving toward requiring firms to demonstrate that AI deployments consider worker impact. Understanding the taxonomy — labor-augmenting versus automating — is the vocabulary you'll need for those conversations.

There is also a genuine business case here that gets underplayed. The expertise-leveling category is where most enterprise AI currently lives, and it's a crowded, commodifying space. The new task-creating category — AI that genuinely enables people to do things they couldn't before — is less populated and potentially more defensible as a competitive position. Products that make people capable of things they couldn't do alone tend to generate stronger loyalty and retention than products that simply make people faster at existing tasks.

## Everything is Designed — Social Media Angle

Here's the dinner party version: every time a company decides to use AI to cut jobs instead of make jobs better, that's not the market speaking — that's a human being making a choice. The economists call this "automation bias," and it's baked into how we measure productivity. We can count the salaries we eliminate. We can't easily count the value of a workforce that's twenty percent smarter or twenty percent more capable. So we keep choosing the thing we can measure, even when it's the worse choice.

The real question "Can AI be pro-worker?" is really "Will anyone decide to build it that way?" That's a design question, not an economics question. And if you believe the answer should be yes, that belief has to show up in what you build — not just how you describe it.
