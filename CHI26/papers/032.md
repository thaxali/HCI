**Title:** Building Pro-Worker Artificial Intelligence
**Authors:** Daron Acemoglu, David Autor, and Simon Johnson
**Source:** The Hamilton Project, Brookings Institution (February 2026)
**One-liner:** Three of the world's most influential economists argue that AI's potential to amplify human expertise is as transformative as its power to automate — and that market failures are causing us to systematically underinvest in the pro-worker version.

---

## TL;DR — Why You Should Care

Here is the core tension at the heart of every AI product decision you will ever make: AI can be built to replace human expertise, or it can be built to extend it. These are not the same thing. They are not even close to the same thing. And which path a company, a product team, or a policy framework chooses has profound consequences — for workers, for inequality, for the economy, and yes, for whether a research platform like Seena adds genuine value or just cheaper noise.

Daron Acemoglu, David Autor, and Simon Johnson — three MIT economists who are among the most cited researchers on technology and labor in the world — have written a rigorous framework for distinguishing pro-worker AI from anti-worker AI. Their paper, published through the Hamilton Project at Brookings in February 2026, is not a manifesto or a screed. It is a careful conceptual taxonomy, backed by labor economics, illustrated with concrete examples, and accompanied by nine specific policy recommendations. But buried inside the academic language is an urgent argument: the market is systematically underproducing AI that makes workers more capable, and we are probably going to regret that.

The stat that should stop you cold: fifty-two percent of U.S. workers surveyed in 2024 were worried about how AI would affect their jobs. More striking — forty-two percent of workers who currently use AI at work believe it will reduce their future job opportunities. That number is substantially higher than the thirty percent of non-AI users who say the same thing. The people closest to the technology are the most worried about it. That tells you something important about what most AI actually feels like to use.

## The Core Argument

The paper's central contribution is a five-part taxonomy of how technologies interact with human labor:

**Labor-augmenting technologies** make workers faster or more productive at tasks they already do — think an electric cable stripper replacing a hand stripper. These are ambiguously pro-worker, because higher productivity can lower prices rather than raise wages.

**Capital-augmenting technologies** do the same for machines — a lighter, better version of the same tool. Also ambiguous.

**Automation technologies** directly substitute machines or algorithms for tasks that humans were doing. These are unambiguously not pro-worker: they reduce labor's share of value-added by reallocating tasks from workers to capital. A cable-installing robot eliminates the need for the human expertise that was previously required.

**New task-creating technologies** are the key category — the one Acemoglu, Autor, and Johnson call unambiguously pro-worker. Technologies like ethernet networks, fiber optic cabling, and occupancy-aware HVAC systems didn't just make electricians faster. They created entirely new categories of work that required entirely new categories of expertise. When new tasks appear, demand for the novel expertise those tasks require increases, which raises wages. These technologies expand the pie of human skill rather than shrinking it.

**Expertise-leveling technologies** are ambiguous — they give less-expert workers access to capabilities that previously required high expertise (think blood oximeters enabling nurses to do what only doctors could previously do), but they can also devalue the expertise of incumbents. Whether they're pro-worker depends heavily on context.

The crucial insight: most of what is being built and hyped in AI right now falls into the automation category. The AGI vision — machines that eventually outperform humans at all economically valuable work — is, almost by definition, the most extreme anti-worker technology imaginable. It commodifies expertise entirely. And yet that is the stated goal of the major AI labs.

## Key Insights and Evaluation

The strongest section of the paper is its account of why the market is failing to produce pro-worker AI. The authors give two explanations, both compelling.

**Misaligned incentives** at the firm level: managers in conflictual labor relationships with unions often prefer automation that reduces their dependence on unionized workers. It's not that automation is more productive — it's that it shifts bargaining power. This is a disturbing mechanism, and it means that the choice between augmenting and automating isn't purely technical or economic. It's political.

**Misaligned incentives** at the developer level: AI companies building toward AGI naturally invest in capabilities that make machines more autonomous, not in capabilities that make human expertise more valuable. The dominant ideology of the field — AGI as the highest possible pursuit — shapes what researchers work on and what investors fund. **Ideology, not just incentives, is steering AI away from workers.**

The authors are appropriately measured about their examples of pro-worker AI that actually exists in the world. The USPTO's "More Like This" AI search tool — adopted by eighty percent of patent examiners by June 2024 — is a clean example. It doesn't replace patent examiners. It makes them dramatically better at finding prior art, which is the core intellectual work of their job. Examiners can now search in seconds what previously took hours, freeing their attention for the judgment-intensive analysis that was always the most valuable part of their expertise. That is the pattern of pro-worker AI: **it amplifies the scarce, judgment-intensive part of expertise, while automating the tedious, time-consuming scaffolding around it.**

Another example is the Empowerment Companion — a personalized conversational AI assistant for low-wage custodial workers at a Pacific Northwest social enterprise that employs people with disabilities. This tool provides on-demand task management, feedback, and guidance. It helps workers complete their work orders correctly, using computer vision to confirm task completion and redirect workers who miss steps. The authors are careful to note that the same technology could be — and in other contexts, is — used to monitor and discipline workers (Amazon Flex being the cautionary contrast). The technology is not inherently pro-worker or anti-worker. The design choices, and who they serve, are what matter.

## Seena Labs Relevance

This paper is almost a description of what Seena is trying to do, written by three of the most credible economists alive.

Think about what Seena's research platform does. It does not replace human researchers. It does not generate "insights" that eliminate the need for a qualitative analyst. What it does — ideally — is amplify the parts of a researcher's expertise that matter most: the judgment about which signals to attend to, the synthesis across dozens of interviews, the translation of observations into actionable product decisions. Seena handles the scaffolding. The researcher handles the expertise.

That is new task-creating technology, not automation technology. And Acemoglu, Autor, and Johnson give you the conceptual vocabulary to explain why that distinction matters — not just ethically, but economically. **The economic argument for Seena is that it makes researcher expertise more valuable, not less necessary.** That is a fundamentally different product promise than "AI does the research for you."

This framing is also directly relevant to how you pitch Seena to enterprise clients who are considering AI research tools. The fear in the market is that AI will commodify insights and devalue research functions. The counter-argument is that AI can instead extend the range and depth of what researchers can do, enabling them to process more signals, surface more patterns, and make better decisions than they could alone. That is the pro-worker AI argument. It is also the argument for why research teams should want to adopt Seena rather than fear it.

There is a sharp design implication here too. The authors distinguish between a tool that "allows anyone to do anything without need of expertise, judgment, or training" — which would fully commodify labor — and a tool that extends expertise, enabling workers to tackle new tasks and develop new capabilities. If Seena's AI gives any brand manager with no research training the ability to generate the same output as a skilled qualitative researcher, that is the anti-worker version of the product. If it makes skilled researchers dramatically more productive and capable, that is the pro-worker version. The design choices that determine which version you build are not accidental. They are intentional. This paper gives you language for making them intentionally and defending those choices to stakeholders.

## Why This Matters

The Industrial Revolution is the cautionary tale running through this paper. Handloom weavers saw their earnings fall by two-thirds in the early decades of the nineteenth century. They were not replaced by an economic force of nature. They were replaced by specific design choices about how to organize textile production — choices that concentrated gains with mill owners and diffused costs across workers. The technology was not inevitable. The distribution of gains was not inevitable. What made it feel inevitable was that no one in a position of power at the time had a framework for seeing it differently.

We are in an analogous moment. The current trajectory of AI development — toward AGI, toward automation, toward commodification of expertise — is not the only possible trajectory. It is the one the market is currently selecting for, for identifiable reasons that include misaligned incentives and ideological capture of the field. The authors argue that public policy can shift those incentives through tax reform, targeted investment in health care and education, antitrust enforcement, worker voice mechanisms, and intellectual property protections for worker expertise.

The policy section is somewhat less gripping than the conceptual framework — that is almost always true of economics papers — but the underlying insight is important: the direction of technological change is not predetermined. It is shaped by choices. And choices can be made differently.

## Everything is Designed — Social Media Angle

Here is the dinner-party version of this paper.

When people worry about AI taking jobs, they usually imagine a machine one day doing everything a human does, better and faster. Acemoglu, Autor, and Johnson say that framing is actually part of the problem — because it treats the outcome as inevitable, when really it is a design choice.

There are two completely different visions of what AI can do. One vision: AI gradually absorbs more and more of what experts know, making it possible to produce expert-level outputs without expert-level humans. Call this the commodification vision. The other vision: AI extends what experts can do, handling the tedious scaffolding so they can focus on the judgment that only humans can provide, and opening up entirely new categories of work that didn't exist before. Call this the collaboration vision.

The economists' punchline is that the market is systematically underproducing the collaboration vision and overproducing the commodification vision — not because collaboration is technically harder, but because the incentives are misaligned and the dominant ideology of the AI field is pointed in the wrong direction.

The implication for anyone building AI products: every product decision about automation versus augmentation is also a values decision. What you build determines what kind of expertise economy you're contributing to. That is not a neutral technical choice. It is a design choice. And if you believe AI should make human expertise more valuable rather than less necessary, that belief has to show up in how you build the product — not just how you describe it.

## Industry vs. Theory

This paper has one limitation worth naming: it is written by labor economists, not AI practitioners or HCI researchers. The taxonomy is conceptually clean, but real products do not fall neatly into a single category. Most AI tools are some mix of automation and augmentation, and the balance shifts depending on who is using them and in what context. The authors acknowledge this — they note that "most real-world technologies do not fall neatly into one category." But the framework is still enormously useful as a direction-of-travel indicator.

The practical applicability is high. This paper gives you a defensible, rigorous framework for articulating the design philosophy behind any AI product — why your product amplifies rather than replaces, why that matters economically, and how to communicate it to both customers and regulators. **In a world where AI ethics is increasingly a board-level concern, having Acemoglu, Autor, and Johnson as your intellectual backing is not nothing.**
